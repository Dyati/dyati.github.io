What Are People Actually Trying to Say?

Words are not the main thing. They are tools. We should focus on what the tools are being used for.

Function is what a message is trying to achieve. If someone complains, the function may be to get help. If someone warns us, the function may be to keep us safe. If someone asks a question, the function may be to gain understanding. The function tells us what the message is trying to do.

Spirit is the reason that function matters. It is the deeper purpose underneath the message. If the function is to warn someone, the spirit may be care. If the function is to correct someone, the spirit may be improvement. If the function is to set a rule, the spirit may be protection, fairness, or order.

Words sit on the surface. Function sits beneath the words. Spirit sits beneath the function.

When we focus only on words, we often miss what matters. A person may choose the wrong phrase, speak while frustrated, or express themselves poorly. If we become trapped by wording, we stop looking for the real purpose of the communication.

The same principle applies to rules. A rule is usually valuable because it serves a purpose. That purpose is its spirit. The wording exists to support that purpose. When people forget the purpose and worship the wording, the rule loses its meaning.

We should train ourselves to ask better questions. Not “What exact words were used?” Not “Can I find a flaw in this sentence?” Instead, we should ask, “What is this trying to achieve?” and “Why does that goal matter?”

If we understand the function and the spirit, we understand what truly matters.
