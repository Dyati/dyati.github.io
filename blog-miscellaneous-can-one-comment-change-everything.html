Can One Comment Change Everything?

I used to play a lot of Tom Clancy's Rainbow Six Siege. Back then, if a player in the team selected Recruit as their character, there was a good chance the rest of the team would also select Recruit. This became a tradition. It was not about strategy. It was about having fun.

Once in a while, a player would not select Recruit even though the rest of the team did. He had not done anything wrong. He just made a different choice. Yet someone in the team would interpret that choice as a problem, and the team usually accepted the interpretation and killed him as soon as the match started.

So I started doing something about it. As soon as I noticed someone would not select Recruit, I would quickly tell the team, before anyone said or did anything, that he is our leader and we should follow him. Nothing comes with built-in meaning. We just assign meaning to whatever happens.

A person who does not follow the group can be interpreted as rude, confident, or a leader. The event stays the same but the story attached to it changes.

The first story usually has an advantage.

We usually do not spend much time analyzing social situations. We just accept the interpretation that is already circulating. Once an interpretation is established, later interpretations have to compete with it. If you can predict a negative interpretation, it is usually easier to replace it before it appears than after it appears.

We usually approach social situations defensively. We wait for somebody to make a negative comment. Then we respond and this works sometimes, but it puts us in the position of arguing against an idea that already exists. It is better to establish a positive interpretation first.

This does not mean every positive interpretation is correct. It also does not mean people should ignore genuine problems.

The goal is to recognize that many social judgments are made before enough information exists to justify them. In those situations, people often default to the most available explanation. Sometimes that explanation is negative.

Look for moments where a negative interpretation is predictable. Then ask “What is another reasonable way to see this?” The alternative interpretation does not need to be profound. In fact, it usually works better when it is simple.

The point is not that these interpretations are true. The point is that they are just as plausible as the negative versions people usually reach for automatically.

This changes how groups behave.

Conflicts usually do not begin with bad intentions. They begin with a story. Someone decides what another person's behavior means, and the group accepts it. Because of that, one of the simplest ways to influence a group is to speak before an interpretation is established.

You do not need authority or a long argument. You just need to provide an interpretation that people can adopt. Many situations are more open than they appear.

Before a group decides what something means, there is a brief moment when multiple interpretations are possible. That moment is usually short. But it is often enough.
