Is Keeping Your Word Overrated?

A commitment is a decision made under limited knowledge. Growth is the discovery of what that knowledge missed. When they conflict, growth deserves priority.

No promise can account for the person you will become. Experience changes values, goals, and understanding. A commitment that once expressed your best judgment may later reflect a narrower version of yourself.

You are a process, not a fixed self.

The value of a commitment depends on what it produces. It has no authority apart from the life it serves. When it strengthens development, it remains useful. When it blocks development, it becomes a constraint.

We often treat commitments as sacred. We remain loyal to careers, relationships, beliefs, and identities long after these cease to foster growth. We preserve continuity at the cost of possibility.

This mistake comes from confusing consistency with integrity. Consistency preserves past choices. Integrity follows present truth. The two are not always the same.

Human flourishing requires change. Learning, mastery, creativity, and wisdom all depend on the ability to revise oneself. A person who cannot abandon outdated commitments cannot fully develop new capacities.

Roles and promises are tools, not ends. Their purpose is to support a meaningful life. When they no longer fulfill that purpose, preserving them becomes an act of self-limitation.

Leaving a commitment is not necessarily a failure. Sometimes it is recognition that growth has revealed a better path. Refusing that path for the sake of an old decision places the past above the future.

A fulfilled life is not measured by unwavering loyalty to every promise ever made. It is measured by the extent to which a person realizes their potential.

Your highest responsibility is your own growth.
