Is Nationalism Dangerous?

Nationalism helped start the two deadliest wars in history.

The core idea is simple: your nation comes first. That belief took hold in the 1800s and dominated global politics by the 1900s.

It fed World War One directly. European groups wanted their own states. Governments stoked national pride and rivalry. A Serbian nationalist killed Archduke Franz Ferdinand in 1914. Alliances kicked in. Armies mobilized. Europe went to war.

It fed World War Two as well. Germany was humiliated after the first war. Hitler channeled that resentment into nationalist energy. He tied national identity to racial superiority. That justified invasion and genocide. Another global war followed.

Nationalism often turned competition into hatred. It split humanity into camps.

That's why it's worth questioning it.

There's also a personal case. Nationalism can become an identity you inherit rather than choose. It rewards belonging over thinking. It makes loyalty feel more important than truth. That's dangerous.

The healthier move is to define yourself by what you do and think, not where you're from. You can appreciate your culture and history without making national identity your whole self. You can borrow ideas from anywhere. You can judge things on merit.

The 20th century showed what happens when nationalism goes unchecked. Moving past it isn't about erasing where you're from. It's about not letting it limit who you become.

You didn't choose your birthplace. You can choose your values. Make that choice consciously. Be proud of what you create, not what you inherit.
