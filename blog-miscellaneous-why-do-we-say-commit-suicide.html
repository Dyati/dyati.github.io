Why Do We Say “Commit Suicide”?

Words carry history. Sometimes they carry judgment.

The word “commit” comes from Latin. It entered English with meanings like entrust, place, or carry out. Over time, it became linked to wrongs. People commit crimes, murder, fraud, sins.

That connection matters.

Words do not merely describe reality. They help shape how we interpret it. The categories built into language become the categories through which we understand the world. A word can frame an act before we have even begun to think about it.

The word “sin” comes from Old English synn. It referred to violation of divine law, offense against God; moral wrongdoing. In Christian Europe, suicide was often treated as a sin. Many religious thinkers believed that life belonged to God. Taking one's own life was seen as rejecting a divine gift. The act was condemned. The language reflected that judgment.

As a result, people began to speak of those who “committed” suicide. The phrase placed suicide in the same category as crimes and offenses. It carried moral weight. It suggested guilt.

What also matters is the framework from which we judge an action. Something considered wrong within one framework may not be considered wrong within another. In some religious frameworks, suicide may be seen as a sin because life is understood as a gift from God. In an existential framework, the focus is on meaning, freedom, and the conditions of human existence.

Every judgment depends on assumptions about what life is, who owns it, and what makes an action right or wrong. When those assumptions change, the meaning of the action can change as well. The same event may appear as a moral failure in one worldview and as a logical human choice in another.

Today, we question that way of speaking.

Modern psychology often views suicide through the lens of suffering rather than sin. Depression, trauma, despair, and isolation are human struggles. Language that sounds accusatory can make those struggles harder to discuss.

Existential philosophy also changed the conversation. Thinkers such as Albert Camus asked whether life has meaning and whether it is worth living. Camus called suicide the central philosophical question.

For that reason, we now prefer phrases such as “died by suicide.” This expression describes an event without placing it inside an outdated moral framework.

Changing a phrase can influence how suffering is understood. Sometimes a single word can shift a conversation from condemnation to compassion. That is why words matter.
