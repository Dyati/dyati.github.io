What Is a Good-Faith Conversation?

What Is a Good-Faith Conversation?

A good-faith conversation happens when people honestly share what they believe, try to understand each other fairly, and stay open to changing their minds when they hear a stronger argument. This idea comes from several traditions, but it rests on a few simple principles.

The oldest idea comes from Roman law. It was called bona fides, meaning “good faith.” It required people to act honestly and not deceive others. They could not hide important facts, exploit loopholes, or follow the letter of an agreement while violating its spirit.

In conversation, this means saying what you truly believe and avoiding tricks, half-truths, or misleading statements.

Philosopher Paul Grice later explained how honest conversations work. He argued that people follow four basic rules: Say what you believe is true, give the right amount of information, stay relevant, be clear and direct.

These rules make conversations useful and trustworthy.

For example, if a coworker asks for feedback on a report, a good-faith response might be: “The data is strong, but the main argument needs more development.” It is honest, focused, relevant, and clear.

By contrast, imagine a manager hiding important information during a salary negotiation to gain an advantage. Even without telling a direct lie, the manager is acting against the spirit of honesty.

Good-faith conversation begins with truthful communication.

Good faith is not only about speaking honestly. It is also about listening fairly.

The Principle of Charity says we should interpret other people's words in the most reasonable way possible. Instead of assuming the worst, we should try to understand what they actually mean.

Philosopher John Stuart Mill argued that even mistaken opinions deserve serious attention. If an idea is wrong, discussing it helps us understand why it is wrong. If it is right, we can learn from it. If it contains some truth, both sides can improve their understanding.

For example, someone in a debate might use an economic term incorrectly. A bad-faith listener focuses on the mistake and ignores the main point. A good-faith listener understands what the speaker is trying to say and responds to the actual argument.

Listening fairly means treating others as people who might have something valuable to contribute.

Even honest and fair people can struggle to have a good-faith conversation if the environment is unfair.

Philosopher Jürgen Habermas argued that conversations work best when everyone has an equal chance to speak, question, and disagree. No one should win because of status, wealth, authority, or intimidation. The better argument should be the only thing that matters.

This ideal is difficult to achieve, but it provides a goal.

A town meeting where everyone gets equal speaking time comes close to this ideal. A peer-review process that judges ideas without knowing who wrote them is another example.

On the other hand, a workplace where employees fear disagreeing with the boss does not meet this standard, even if they are technically allowed to speak.

Good-faith conversation depends on fair conditions as well as good intentions.

Philosopher Jean-Paul Sartre explored the opposite of good faith: bad faith.

Bad faith happens when people avoid responsibility for their beliefs and choices. Instead of owning their views, they hide behind roles, excuses, or performances.

A debater who says, “I'm just playing devil's advocate,” to avoid defending a position may be acting in bad faith. So might an executive who blames “company policy” for a decision they personally supported.

Bad faith can look like genuine discussion. Someone may ask questions, nod politely, and appear engaged while having no intention of reconsidering their position.

For example, a politician may hold a public forum after already making a final decision. A person in an argument may repeatedly say, “You might be right,” while never seriously considering another viewpoint.

Real good faith requires admitting that you could be wrong and allowing new ideas to change your mind.

A good-faith conversation combines all of these qualities: honesty about what you believe, cooperation in how you communicate, fairness in how you interpret others, openness to new evidence and better arguments, equal opportunities for everyone to speak, authenticity and personal responsibility.

None of these are easy. People misunderstand each other. Bias gets in the way. Power differences distort discussions. Self-deception is common.

That is why good-faith conversation is not something that happens automatically. It is a standard we choose to pursue.

When people practice it, conversations create understanding and help everyone think more clearly. When they do not, conversations become performances, power struggles, or wasted opportunities.

Let's have more good-faith conversations.
