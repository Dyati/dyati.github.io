Did Women Help Build Their Own Oppression?

What do you need to cut down a tree? An axe. What is an axe made of? Steel and wood. Wood helps cut down wood.

Social systems often work the same way. They are not maintained by one group alone. The people inside them help keep them alive.

When discussing women's rights, we often focus only on men. Men often held political power, wrote laws, and controlled institutions. This is true. Yet it is only part of the story.

Mary Wollstonecraft pointed out in A Vindication of the Rights of Woman that women are not naturally inferior to men. They appeared inferior because society denied them education and opportunity. She pointed out that women are capable of reason, action, and changing society.

This means social systems survive because many people participate in them. Some enforce the rules. Others accept them. Both help preserve the system.

Gender roles emerged for practical reasons. For most of history, survival was difficult. Physical strength mattered more than it does today. A division of labor helped societies survive.

But conditions changed.

Education became widely available. Modern economies depend more on knowledge than strength. The reasons that once justified rigid gender roles became weaker.

Yet the roles remained.

We still tell men how to act. We still tell women how to act. These expectations often limit us more than they help us.

The goal should not be a struggle between men and women. The goal should be freedom from outdated categories. Gender roles may have helped our ancestors survive. Today, they often prevent us from becoming who we want to be.

We should judge each other as individuals, not as genders.
