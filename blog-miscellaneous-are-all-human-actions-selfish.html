Are All Human Actions Selfish?

Many of us heard the idea that people would do something only if it benefited them. Every action can be traced back to self-interest. I found this view acceptable since it offers a neat explanation for human behavior.

We often disagree about what is true. A good way to judge an explanation is to ask whether it can be tested. This is called falsifiability. A claim is falsifiable if we can imagine evidence that would prove it wrong. Consider the claim that all crows are black. If we find even a single white crow, the claim fails. This makes it a useful claim. It risks being wrong.

Ask what counts as evidence that someone acted from genuine concern for another person? If the answer is “nothing,” then the claim that all actions are selfish is unfalsifiable. It cannot be tested because it is designed to fit every outcome.

We have to be cautious about accepting this claim. It is a belief that protects itself from criticism.

In addition, the belief that people would do something only for personal gain shapes how we see others.

When we assume that acts of kindness hide selfish motives, it becomes hard to trust, generosity will be seen as fake, loyalty as strategy, compassion as disguise. The world appears cold.

A better view is that people are driven by many motives such as love, compassion, curiosity, duty, loyalty, and the search for meaning. Self-interest is one of the motives, but it is not the only one.

Human motivation is complex. We do not need to believe that people are saints. We do not need to believe that people are purely selfish either. People are capable of both self-interest and concern for others.

The temptation to reduce everything to one explanation is understandable. Simple explanations are attractive. They make the world easier to understand. Yet reality is often more complicated.
