Are Your Values Actually Yours?

How do you draw a house? Draw a house on a paper or screen. At what age did you learn to draw that kind of house? Probably childhood. You probably never considered learning to draw a house in other ways.

That is how most values work.

Almost all of us inherit values from our environment. We become attached to these values and we feel that they are part of our identity. We rarely, if ever, challenge these values. Some of us do not even know the assumptions on which these values stand.

Ultimately, we cannot really know anything with absolute certainty. Some people agree on some assumptions and build values on them, while others agree on other assumptions and build other values on them.

Most of us often choose one of two extreme stances: naïve realism (”My assumptions are the correct assumptions”) or relativism (”Since assumptions can't be proven, all assumptions are equally valid”). One closes the mind. The other collapses into incoherence.

Perspectivism sits between these extremes. No single view captures the whole truth. But that does not mean all views are equal. Some are more examined, more tested, more coherent than others. The goal is not to find the one correct worldview and stop. The goal is to keep examining, revising, and expanding your values as new perspectives emerge.

But you cannot examine what you have never encountered. Exposure is the prerequisite. Seek out people, books, films, and ideas from cultures far outside your own — not as tourism, but as raw material for rethinking. The friction of genuine difference is what forces assumptions into the open.

Most of us never get there. History shows what that costs.

Foot binding. Witch hunts. Sati. Human sacrifice. Feudalism. Child labor. Caste discrimination. Racial segregation. Colonialism. Slavery. Each of these was once defended, normalized, and believed in by ordinary people like us who thought they were living rightly.

That should unsettle us about what is considered normal today.

Most of us never push past what Lawrence Kohlberg called conventional morality — following rules because that is what society expects. Robert Kegan mapped similar territory with what he called the socialized mind: a self shaped almost entirely by the values of its surrounding culture, with little ability to step back and evaluate them. Both frameworks describe the same trap — a stage of growth that stops at “this is how things are done” and never asks why or whether it should be.

The difference that matters is between inheriting and choosing.

Inheritance is passive. It happens to you. Choosing is active. Choosing is freedom — examining alternatives, sitting with discomfort, and arriving at values that are actually yours. That freedom depends on having alternatives to examine in the first place. Read writers your community does not read. Travel to places that unsettle your assumptions. Talk to people whose lives look nothing like yours. The wider your exposure, the larger the set of values you actually get to choose from.

Question one thing you have never questioned. Draw a different house.
